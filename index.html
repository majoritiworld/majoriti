00 ‚Äî Context

Why this report exists

We set out to understand what communities actually are ‚Äî and what holds them back. Not from textbooks, but from the people building them with their own hands, time, and often their own money.

We interviewed 12 community leaders across Israel, Latin America, and the U.S. Their communities range from a slime mold enthusiast group to one of the world's top scientific institutions. From a fitness movement in Panama City to a Kabbalah study circle over Zoom. From a university alumni network to a food rescue nonprofit.

What they share is more interesting than what separates them. Every single one described a moment of accidental discovery ‚Äî a realization that what they were building was bigger than the project, the class, or the event. It was a living thing.

This report maps the landscape, names the pain, and asks the questions that emerged from those conversations.

01 ‚Äî The Landscape

A taxonomy of communities

No two communities we spoke to were alike. But patterns emerged. We found that communities tend to organize along two axes: what draws people in (the purpose) and what holds them up (the structure). Here are the four types of community we identified.

üî•

Mission-Driven

Built around a cause, a belief system, or a desire to change something in the world. The community exists because of the mission ‚Äî and often dies without it.

Robin Food ¬∑ Bet-El ¬∑ KY Kabbalah ¬∑ Lazos
üî¨

Knowledge-Driven

Built around shared curiosity, learning, or expertise. Members come to know ‚Äî and stay because knowing together is better than knowing alone.

Slime Mold ¬∑ HCI ¬∑ Weizmann ¬∑ Meaning of Life ¬∑ Compliance LATAM
‚ö°

Experience-Driven

Built around a shared activity ‚Äî the doing is the bonding. The community is what happens in the spaces between reps, runs, and rounds.

Sweat Club ¬∑ Weavy (product community)
ü™û

Identity-Driven

Built around belonging to a group ‚Äî alumni, diaspora, a shared background. You don't join for what they do, but for who you are when you're there.

NAA ¬∑ Lazos (diaspora dimension)
üîç Pattern we noticed

Most communities are hybrids. Lazos is both mission-driven and identity-driven. Slime Mold started as knowledge-driven but became deeply experience-driven through in-person field tours. The healthiest communities seem to operate in at least two quadrants ‚Äî giving members multiple reasons to stay.

02 ‚Äî Leader Types

Four kinds of community leaders

We didn't find "community managers." We found people who fell into a role that doesn't have a good name. Some built something from nothing; others inherited a living organism and had to keep it breathing. Here's what we found.

üå±

The Accidental Founder

"I didn't think I was building a community when I started."

They started a Facebook group, organized one tour, cooked one meal ‚Äî and suddenly people showed up and kept showing up. They learned community by doing it, not studying it. Their superpower is authenticity. Their kryptonite is burnout.

üèõ

The Institutional Steward

"The community existed before me and will exist after me."

They manage a community inside a larger organization ‚Äî a university, an institute, a global network. They have resources but also constraints. They navigate bureaucracy to create belonging. Their superpower is infrastructure. Their kryptonite is distance from the members.

üî•

The Mission Carrier

"You are a servant of the community, not the owner."

The community is inseparable from their personal calling ‚Äî spiritual, social, moral. They attract people through conviction. Their greatest fear is arrogance; their greatest strength is that people come for the message and stay for the messenger. They worry about what happens when they leave.

üîå

The Connector-Operator

"Being the most intense person in the room ‚Äî constantly."

They focus on logistics, growth, and follow-up. They track attendance in spreadsheets, create WhatsApp groups for "active members," and personally message every attendee for feedback. Their superpower is execution. Their kryptonite is that the operational work eats the strategic work.

Every leader we interviewed wore at least two of these hats. The question isn't which type you are ‚Äî it's which hat is missing.

03 ‚Äî The 6 Pain Clusters

What keeps community leaders up at night

Across 12 very different communities, we kept hearing the same six tensions. These aren't just "problems to solve" ‚Äî they're structural paradoxes that every community must navigate. Here they are, in the leaders' own words.

üß©
Cluster 1

Everyone wants to lead until it's time to lead

On leadership transitions, burnout, and the fragility of one-person dependencies

The most universal pain: communities are built on the shoulders of one or two individuals, and that model is inherently fragile. When the leader burns out, moves away, or tries to hand off ‚Äî things break.

"She came to this place thinking 'it's a mess, I'll take it from here, I'll clean it up.' Famous last words."

‚Äî Leader, Robin Food (on a failed leadership transition)

One leader described building an entire fundraising operation and training a team for a year before stepping back ‚Äî only to watch the successor disregard the institutional knowledge and drive the organization to closure in six months.

I don't fully feel like a leader yet... I'm afraid of arrogance, of becoming the self-proclaimed spiritual master.

‚Äî Leader, KY Kabbalah
üîç Pattern we noticed

The leaders who were most afraid of being irreplaceable were often the most irreplaceable. Humility about the leadership role paradoxically made succession harder ‚Äî because they never formalized what they brought to the community that wasn't just "content."

If the community dies when the leader leaves ‚Äî was it ever really a community, or was it a following?
üå±
Cluster 2

Nobody planned this ‚Äî and that's the magic

On emergent leadership, organic growth, and the beauty of accidental communities

Almost every leader told us the same thing: they didn't set out to build a community. It happened. A Facebook group for sharing slime mold photos. A cooking project that attracted volunteers. A student club that became something bigger than the curriculum.

"I didn't think that I was building a community when I started. It just started as an online group for sharing knowledge."

‚Äî Leader, Slime Mold Community

What's remarkable is how leaders described the moment of recognition ‚Äî the point where they realized they had something alive on their hands. For one, it was a field tour where eight strangers shared an electric moment of curiosity. For another, it was watching repeat faces at what were supposed to be one-off events.

"Community was always a means for an end for me. I wanted things that I thought should exist, to exist. And one of the main ways to make that happen was to get people excited and working together."

‚Äî Leader, Robin Food

The emergent quality extends to leadership within communities too. One leader described a co-leader who, over the course of three meetings, went from participant to organizer to preparing material independently ‚Äî completely unprompted.

üîç Pattern we noticed

The communities that grew most organically were the ones where the leader's enthusiasm was contagious, not their authority. People didn't join because they were told to ‚Äî they joined because they felt something was happening and didn't want to miss it.

Can you design emergence ‚Äî or only design the conditions for it?

Every leader we spoke to ‚Äî without exception ‚Äî described a gap between what their community could be and what it actually is. Not out of dissatisfaction, but out of a kind of aching awareness that the potential is enormous and the resources are not.

Cluster 4

On communication channels, the platform problem, and reaching people where they are

Every single community we interviewed relies on WhatsApp as their primary coordination tool. Every. Single. One. They also all know it's not ideal. They tried Slack (nobody engaged), email (nobody read), and custom platforms (too much friction). So WhatsApp wins by default.

"We tried Slack and it was hard. Nobody was engaged on the platform. Even emails ‚Äî not an option. So we said, OK, let's open a WhatsApp group."

‚Äî Leader, HCI Community

Some leaders have gotten creative. One fitness community created a small "Active Members" WhatsApp group of 30 repeat attendees ‚Äî a VIP tier within the community. One rabbi shares his personal WhatsApp with the entire congregation. A tech product community built a 20,000-member Discord as a complement.

The communication problem is really two problems. First: how to reach people (broadcast). Second: how to make people feel reached (connection). The ones who come closest use WhatsApp for intimacy and Instagram or Facebook for broadcast ‚Äî but that means managing multiple channels with no integration.

The tool you use to talk to your community shapes the kind of community you can build.

One leader described struggling to explain why their local chapter matters when they themselves don't fully connect with the international parent organization. Another described the difficulty of naming what they do at all ‚Äî is it a class? A circle? A club? A movement?

The communities with the strongest identities were those with a clear aesthetic, language, or ritual ‚Äî not just a clear mission. Robin Food had puns and a playful visual style. On recurring participation, lurkers, the commitment curve, and the async problem

This is the most operationally painful cluster. Leaders described the anxiety of planning events and not knowing who will show up ‚Äî and the deeper existential question of whether one-time attendees are "members" at all.

Several leaders pointed to the tension between in-person depth and asynchronous breadth. In-person events create magic but exclude people who can't attend. Online channels create access but rarely depth. The best communities need both ‚Äî but building both is twice the work.

"It's not bad to have disengaged people ‚Äî at some point they can become engaged. Even if they're lurking from the outside, they are giving energy to this movement."

One community tracks attendance in a spreadsheet and graduates repeat attendees into a VIP WhatsApp group. Another set fixed meeting times and accepted they'd lose some interested people ‚Äî because trying to accommodate everyone meant accommodating no one.

üîç Pattern we noticed

The leaders who stressed least about participation were the ones who embraced the commitment curve ‚Äî accepting that for every active leader, you need an audience of passive members, and that this is natural, not a failure. The shift from "why isn't everyone participating equally?" to "how do I design multiple levels of engagement?" was the single most liberating reframe in our research.

What if lurkers aren't a problem to solve ‚Äî but the foundation that makes active participation possible?

04 ‚Äî What we take from here

Five things we'll remember

1

Communities are accidents that need infrastructure. Every community we studied emerged organically ‚Äî then hit a wall when it needed structure. The challenge isn't starting communities. It's keeping them alive past the founder's energy.

2

The tools don't exist yet. Communities are using business tools (Trello, Google Sheets) and social tools (WhatsApp, Instagram) because community tools haven't been invented. This is a design opportunity the size of an entire industry.

3

Leaders overestimate how much others want to lead. Expecting equal participation is "naive and anti-natural." The healthiest communities design a spectrum of engagement ‚Äî from lurker to co-leader ‚Äî and make every level feel valued.

4

Identity is felt, not written. The communities with the strongest belonging weren't the ones with the best mission statement. They were the ones with the most distinctive vibe ‚Äî a language, a ritual, an aesthetic that members could carry with them.

5

A community's value exists even for those who never show up. The mere knowledge that a community exists ‚Äî that there are people who care about the same things ‚Äî has value. Building for the lurkers isn't a consolation prize. It's building for the majority.

This is a living document. The research continues with member interviews and a deeper analysis of community structures. If you're a leader who sees yourself in these pages ‚Äî we'd love to hear from you.

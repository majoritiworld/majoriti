Why this report

We're living in what many are calling a burnout society: tired, polarized, and spending most of our idle time scrolling through feeds that leave us emptier than before. At Majoriti, we firmly believe community is the antidote… but only if we understand what makes it so hard to build and sustain.

Across every platform we visit daily (hourly?), one word keeps showing up: community.

But much of what we call "community" today is really just a means to an end. Email lists in exchange for "free" content. Influencers who talk to us like we're their friends. Companies that give us merch... but it's all just a sales funnel disguised as belonging.

The thing is... humans crave community because they crave belonging, agency, and purpose. And real communities can offer all three — better than almost anything else.

But real communities are hard. They're messy, understructured, and almost always running on the energy of one or two people who figured things out as they went.

We wanted to understand what's actually going on. Not from textbooks but from the people building them with their own hands, time, and often their own money.

What we did

We interviewed 13 community leaders across Israel, Latin America, and the U.S. Their communities range from a slime mold enthusiast group to one of the world's top scientific institutions. From a fitness movement in Panama City to a Kabbalah study circle over Zoom. From a university alumni network to a food rescue nonprofit.

What they share is more interesting than what separates them. Every single one described a moment of accidental discovery... a realization that what they were building was bigger than the project, the class, or the event. It was a living thing.

This report maps what we found.

01 — The Landscape

What is a community?

Before getting into what we found, let's start with something that sounds obvious but… clearly isn't. What even is a community?

For every person you ask, you probably will get a different answer. Some people believe communities are just groups of people who share something in common, let's say, a religion, or even geographic proximity like a neighborhood. Others believe communities must have a shared mission that unites them.

Finding a definition

In order to answer this elusive question, I've delved into a lot of reading. If I would have to highlight two books that really helped me answer this question, it would be The Art of Community by Charles H. Vogl, and The Business of Belonging, by David Spinks.

It was actually in Spinks' book that I found one of the most accepted definitions of what a community is, which was coined in 1986 by McMillan and Chavis in their now famous Sense of Community Theory.

Community is the feeling of belonging, mattering, and shared faith that members have in a group.

It's interesting that they say it's a feeling — however, it's a feeling that is held by clear boundaries and pillars. They broke these down into four elements: feeling of belonging, sense of mattering, fulfillment of needs, and shared emotional connection.

It's almost 40 years old… and it still holds up. And the ugly truth is that very few communities we spoke to provide these 4 elements to their members.

The Spectrum of Belonging

What brings people into a community is not the same for everyone. We found it helpful to think of it as a spectrum. We could say this is our contribution to the still emerging field of community research.

Emergent belonging

On one end, we found emergent belonging. Communities that are 100% chosen. You as a leader almost stumble into them. You find out that other people care about this weird thing you care about… and suddenly there's a group. Someone finds an online community of people photographing fungi in the forest and thinks… wait, this is my thing. A philosophy student starts a study circle and people just keep showing up. The belonging is active, discovered, sometimes accidental. These are real stories we heard in our interviews.

Given belonging

On the other end of the spectrum, we found given belonging. Communities you're part of by osmosis. The clearest example: you graduated from a program, so now you're alumni. You didn't seek it out. It was handed to you.

Identity-based belonging

In the middle… identity-based belonging. These are communities in which potential members share something with a group of people (a background, a pain point, a profession) and when a community forms around that, belonging tends to click fast. A group of immigrants in a new country who all share the same struggle of building a network from zero. A group of creative freelancers already living in the same professional world that find a product that solves their needs. They didn't discover each other by accident… but they also didn't get a membership card. The community just made visible something they already had in common.

One last thing

David Spinks, in The Business of Belonging, said something that really changed my perspective on communities.

People come for the utility, but stay for the unity.

We want to believe, or at least me being extremely idealistic, that people are all around looking for communities and belonging. But the truth is, people mostly have pain points that need solutions, and these tend to be the perfect entry points to communities. So as community leaders, we need to focus more on the utility we bring to our members and truly understand their pain points, and then build an environment so safe and inviting that people can go from utility to unity — from passive members to engaged ones that drive the community forward.

02 — Leader Types

Four kinds of community leaders

We didn't find "community managers." We found people who fell into a role that doesn't have a good name. Some built something from nothing; others inherited a living organism and had to keep it breathing. Here's what we found.

The Accidental Founder

"I didn't think I was building a community when I started."

They started a Facebook group, organized one tour, cooked one meal — and suddenly people showed up and kept showing up. They learned community by doing it, not studying it. Their superpower is authenticity. Their kryptonite is burnout.

The Institutional Steward

"The community existed before me and will exist after me."

They manage a community inside a larger organization — a university, an institute, a global network. They have resources but also constraints. They navigate bureaucracy to create belonging. Their superpower is infrastructure. Their kryptonite is distance from the members.

The Mission Carrier

"You are a servant of the community, not the owner."

The community is inseparable from their personal calling — spiritual, social, moral. They attract people through conviction. Their greatest fear is arrogance; their greatest strength is that people come for the message and stay for the messenger. They worry about what happens when they leave.

The Connector-Operator

"Being the most intense person in the room — constantly."

They focus on logistics, growth, and follow-up. They track attendance in spreadsheets, create WhatsApp groups for "active members," and personally message every attendee for feedback. Their superpower is execution. Their kryptonite is that the operational work eats the strategic work.

Every leader we interviewed wore at least two of these hats. The question isn't which type you are — it's which hat is missing.

03 — The 6 Pain Clusters

What keeps community leaders up at night

Across 13 very different communities, we kept hearing the same six tensions. These aren't just "problems to solve" — they're structural paradoxes that every community must navigate. Here they are, in the leaders' own words.

Cluster 1

You want to burn out? Become a community leader.

On leadership dependency, burnout, and the fragility of one-person operations

Almost every leader we spoke to described the same problem: the community runs on them. Because communities tend to be less structured than companies, they heavily depend on leaders to do most of the work — and leaders end up carrying far more than is sustainable.

"I would sit and have like an hour long chat with every new volunteer, tell them the story of Robin food, get to know their story, plug them into the system, coordinate expectations and find a way for them to fit in... I wore very many hats."

— Founder, mission-driven social enterprise

One leader described doing food rescues at 6:00 AM before events, managing volunteers, running a restaurant, and handling everything in between. Another spoke about the "crazy overload" of trying to personally respond to every community member while keeping the community feel personal.

"Overload, crazy overload, crazy overload. As the volume goes up, it's very hard to keep it personal. In the beginning we were there all the time. Now I get there like once in 2-3 days. It's not enough... We are loaded with work."

— Founder, product-led creator community

This isn't just exhaustion. It's structural fragility. When one person holds all the knowledge, all the relationships, all the context — the community becomes inseparable from that person's energy.

Pattern we noticed

Leadership burnout in communities isn't a personal failure — it's a structural inevitability when one person carries everything. Without clear role distribution, documentation, or systems to distribute responsibility, leaders find themselves "treading water" just to keep things alive. The more successful the community, the heavier the burden becomes.

If the community collapses the moment you step away, you haven't built a community — you've built a dependency.
Cluster 2

Some members want to do more — but there's nowhere for them to go

On emergent leadership, informal core groups, and the invisible hierarchy of engagement

In almost every community we studied, a small group of members naturally stepped up. They wanted to contribute, lead workshops, take on projects. This is the beautiful reality of communities — people want to help.

"Right now I have about three students who take initiative. One of them is at this point a co-leader... That's what I want to see for everyone. I want it to be a communal creation."

— Founder, philosophy-driven student community

Another described how their small core group of active members emerged entirely organically — people talking to each other without the founders, creating connections on their own. Members would come up and say they knew something and would like to lead a workshop.

But most communities didn't have a way to recognize, formalize, or support this energy.

Original — Spanish

"Lo que más me cuesta a mí es intentar armar una estructura... Tener que armar una mesa con voluntarios. Pero a la vez todavía no conozco tanto a los miembros como para decir, bueno, vení y lo hagamos juntos."

What's hardest for me is trying to build a structure... Having to build a team with volunteers. But I still don't know the members well enough to say, come, let's do this together.

— Leader, identity-based Latin American community

One leader had a term for these people: "bangers" — but none of the bangers knew they were bangers. The roles existed in practice but not in name or structure.

Pattern we noticed

Emergent leadership is happening everywhere — but it's invisible, informal, and fragile. The most engaged members don't have a pathway from "participant" to "contributor" to "co-leader." Without tooling or visibility, this energy either burns out or gets absorbed by the founder, who becomes the bottleneck once again.

If you're manually activating every single leader in your community, you don't have a system — you have a heroic effort that can't scale.
Cluster 3

We know what we could do — we just can't do it

On missed potential, stalled projects, and the gap between vision and capacity

Leaders described ambitious projects that never got off the ground. A scientific community wanted to publish a book documenting their research — members were enthusiastic, the content was there, but the project stalled and never succeeded.

"We talk about doing it for years, but it never happens."

— Leader, knowledge-driven citizen science community

The bottleneck wasn't desire. It wasn't even funding in most cases. It was time, coordination, and bandwidth.

Original — Spanish

"Lo que queremos para el 2026 es no ser eventos aislados, sino generar programas."

What we want for 2026 is to stop being isolated events and start creating programs.

— Leader, identity-based Latin American community

This leader articulated what many felt: the desire to move from one-off events to sustained programs, from transactional moments to relational depth. But without the infrastructure to make that leap, communities stay stuck in reactive mode — responding to immediate needs rather than building toward a vision.

Pattern we noticed

The most value-creating work in communities — relationship maintenance, emotional labor, one-on-one outreach — is invisible, unrecognized, and unscalable. Yet it's essential for survival. Communities want to move from events to programs, from transactional to relational, but they don't have the infrastructure to make that leap.

Ambition without infrastructure isn't vision — it's burnout waiting to happen.
Cluster 4

WhatsApp is king

On the default communication stack, fragmented channels, and tools built for something else

With one exception (a startup community using Discord), every single community we studied relied heavily on WhatsApp as their primary coordination tool.

"We tried Slack… nobody was engaged… email is not an option… so we opened a WhatsApp group."

— Leader, institution-anchored professional community

Why? Not because WhatsApp is great for community management. Because it's universal, it's simple, and nothing better exists that people actually use.

"The infrastructure was very basic. It's just WhatsApp for coordination, Google Sheets for timetables and the CRM and everything. Sure, maybe we could have developed some custom tool, but the more custom it is, the more friction in getting it in people's hands."

— Founder, mission-driven social enterprise

Communities need low friction — but what are the costs of using tools that weren't built for them? WhatsApp doesn't hold memory. It doesn't track roles or progression. It can't distinguish between a lurker and a core contributor. It's a communication tool being forced to be a community tool.

Pattern we noticed

The tool you use to talk to your community shapes the kind of community you can build. Broadcast-only channels create dependency on the founder. One-on-one nudging creates bottlenecks. WhatsApp groups create noise but not structure. Leaders want dialogue, not just announcements — but the tools they have make dialogue nearly impossible at scale.

The tool you use to talk to your community shapes the kind of community you can build.
Cluster 5

We know what we do — but not how to say it

On the lack of a unifying story, identity confusion, and the naming problem

Multiple leaders told us their members wouldn't even describe themselves as "part of a community." They come to events, they value what they receive — but the sense of belonging hasn't crystallized into an identity.

Original — Spanish

"Yo creo que hoy en día los miembros no se sienten parte de una comunidad aún, sino que dirían que me aporta herramientas o espacios, pero así como más aislados."

I think today the members don't feel like they're part of a community yet — they'd say it gives them tools or spaces, but in a more isolated way.

— Leader, identity-based Latin American community

There's a disconnect between what leaders envision and what members experience. Leaders see a community; members see useful events. Leaders have a story in their heads; members don't know they're part of one.

This disconnect becomes even more problematic when trying to bring new people into leadership positions. The community's DNA — its culture, values, and way of doing things — lives entirely in the founder's head.

"It's not just transferring a role — it's transferring the soul, a vibe, a way of doing things and of course, established relationships with large amounts of people."

— Founder, mission-driven social enterprise

When everything that defines the community is tacit knowledge, onboarding becomes nearly impossible. How do you hand over something you can't articulate?

Pattern we noticed

The communities with the strongest identities were those with a clear aesthetic, language, or ritual — not just a clear mission. A social enterprise had puns and a playful visual style. A wellness community had "challenges" as a branded concept. A citizen science group had the organism itself as a metaphor for how they organize. Identity isn't just a mission statement. It's how a community feels from the inside.

If your members can't explain your community in one sentence to a friend, you don't have a story yet.
Cluster 6

They came once — how do we make them come back?

On recurring participation, lurkers, the commitment curve, and the async problem

This is the most operationally painful cluster. Leaders described the anxiety of planning events and not knowing who will show up — and the deeper existential question of whether one-time attendees are "members" at all.

Original — Spanish

"Si no hacía la convocatoria uno a uno, había cero participantes inscriptos."

If I didn't do outreach one-on-one, there were zero people signed up.

— Leader, identity-based Latin American community

The manual labor of activating people — sending individual messages, personal reminders, one-on-one nudges — becomes the only way to get anyone to show up. But this doesn't scale, and it's exhausting.

Several leaders pointed to the tension between in-person depth and asynchronous breadth. In-person events create magic but exclude people who can't attend. Online channels create access but rarely depth. The best communities need both — but building both is twice the work.

"How do we help people understand that their absence is noticed and their showing up is appreciated?"

— Founder, philosophy-driven student community

One community tracks attendance in a spreadsheet and graduates repeat attendees into a VIP WhatsApp group. Another set fixed meeting times and accepted they'd lose some interested people — because trying to accommodate everyone meant accommodating no one.

Pattern we noticed

When the community's value proposition isn't clear — when members don't understand what they're getting or why they should keep coming back — leaders resort to manual activation instead of organic pull. This is a community-market-fit problem. Without a clear, consistent utility that members can articulate, recurring participation becomes dependent on the leader's hustle rather than the community's inherent value.

If you can't articulate what's the value of your community, can you expect members to do so?

04 — What we take from here

Five things we'll remember

1

Most communities emerge organically — then hit a wall when they need structure. Many communities in our research didn't start with an intentional "community plan." They emerged around a shared need or passion. The challenge isn't starting communities. It's keeping them alive past the founder's energy.

2

Community leaders are intuitively choosing tools that aren't designed for their needs. The proof is in the pudding: when communities grow or when leaders need to step away, the limitations become painfully clear. While dedicated community tools exist, they haven't achieved widespread adoption. There's a reason WhatsApp is king — it doesn't work the best, but it works good enough to keep communities alive.

3

Leaders overestimate how much others want to lead. Expecting equal participation from all members is naive and anti-natural. The healthiest communities design a spectrum of engagement — from passive members to co-leaders — and make every level feel valued.

4

Communities struggle when their value isn't clear. When members can't articulate why they come back — when the utility of the community isn't obvious — recurring participation becomes dependent on the leader's manual effort rather than organic pull. This is a community-market-fit problem: if the leader can't clearly explain what their community offers, members won't be able to either.

5

A community's value exists even for those who never show up. It's natural that only a small percentage of members will engage actively. But passive members are still members, and they can become active at any point. The mere knowledge that a community exists — that there are people who care about the same things — has value. Building for the lurkers isn't a consolation prize. They are actually the foundation of most strong communities.
